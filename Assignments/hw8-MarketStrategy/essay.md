### Ans

**a. What are the two major activities of marketing strategy?**

Marketing strategy involves two major activities: **(i) selecting a target market and determining the desired positioning of a product in the minds of the target customers**, and **(ii) specifying the plan for the marketing activities to achieve the desired positioning**.

**b. What are the elements of, and what do we hope to learn from, The 5 C’s marketing analysis?**

The 5 C's of marketing analysis are:
*   **Customers**: Understanding customer needs is crucial. This involves identifying what needs the firm seeks to satisfy through its product or service.
*   **Company**: Analyzing the company's skills and competencies is important. This includes identifying the unique skills and resources that the company possesses to meet customer needs.
*   **Competition**: Identifying who competes with the firm in meeting customer needs. Analyzing competitors' strengths and weaknesses is vital for differentiation.
*   **Collaborators**: Determining who the firm should enlist to help and how to motivate them. This includes channel partners and suppliers.
*   **Context**: Understanding cultural, technological, and legal factors that might limit what is possible. This involves analyzing the broader environment to identify threats, opportunities, and resources.

The 5 C’s analysis is meant to provide a foundation for marketing decision-making by informing the selection of target markets, product positioning, and the marketing mix. This analysis also helps the firm to understand how it can create and capture value.

**c. What are the elements of The 4 P’s marketing mix, and how is the make-up of each element determined?**

The 4 P's of the marketing mix are:
*   **Product**: This refers to the total package of benefits that a customer obtains, not just the physical product. This includes things like the brand name, company reputation, pre-sale education, post-sale support, and availability. Decisions include product line breadth, length and depth, as well as individual item decisions. New product development should follow a sequential process including opportunity identification, design, testing, introduction, and life cycle management.
*   **Place (Channels of Distribution)**: This involves the mechanisms through which a firm "goes to market". Key decisions include channel design (direct, indirect, or dual distribution) and channel management. It's critical to understand customer requirements and the functions that the channel must perform, such as product information, customization, quality assurance, lot size, assortment, availability, service, and logistics.
*   **Promotion (Communications Strategy)**: This refers to the methods used to communicate with customers to build awareness, knowledge, interest, trial, and repeat purchases. It includes both personal selling and non-personal activities like advertising, sales promotion, and public relations. The "6 M's" model (Market, Mission, Message, Media, Money, Measurement) guides planning communications strategy. Understanding the decision-making unit (DMU) is key, as is using different elements of the communication mix to address different issues pertinent to DMU members.
*  **Pricing**: Pricing is a way for the firm to capture the value it has created for customers. The perceived value represents the maximum price a customer is willing to pay. Cost acts as a floor, and the firm must consider marketing objectives (e.g., skimming vs. penetration). Pricing can be customized by the firm by varying prices for different customers.

The make-up of each element of the 4 P’s is determined by the target market, the desired positioning, and an understanding of the customer. The "marketing mix—product, price, place, and promotion—is essentially the working out of the tactical details of the positioning strategy".

### Ans


**a. What is meant by the “objective value” and the “perceived value” of a product? How do these values relate to the price that a company may charge for the product?**

*   **Objective value**, also called true economic value (TEV), is a measure of the benefits a product delivers to a consumer relative to other products, regardless of whether the consumer recognizes those benefits. It is often assessed by understanding a potential buyer's cost structure and considering the competitive alternatives, their prices, and their performance. The objective value is calculated as: **TEV = Cost of the Next-Best Alternative + Value of Performance Differential**.
*   **Perceived value** is the value a consumer *understands* the product to deliver. This can be influenced by the firm’s marketing efforts to communicate the product's benefits. The perceived value of a product to a consumer should equal the maximum price that the consumer is willing to pay for the product.

The price a company charges for a product should ideally fall between its cost of goods sold (COGS) and the perceived value to the customer. By pricing in this range, the firm creates an incentive for both the firm to sell the product and the consumer to purchase it. The objective value and perceived value act as important factors that a company uses to set the price of a product.

**b. With respect to pricing, what provides incentive for a consumer to purchase a product? What provides incentive for a company to produce a product?**

*   A **consumer's incentive to purchase** a product is the difference between the perceived value of the product and its price. The consumer has an incentive to purchase the product when the perceived value is greater than the price.
*   A **company's incentive to produce and sell** a product is the difference between the price it charges and its cost of goods sold (COGS). In order to stay in business and make a positive return, a firm must charge a price that covers its cost of production.

**c. Examine the five “typical” questions listed on page 5 of the article which might be asked in a direct-response pricing survey. The first and fourth questions are valid, but the second, third and fifth questions violate accepted rules for price testing and should not be used. At the bottom of that page, a survey method is discussed including an example of results in Table B. This survey uses a valid method. Think about why the second, third and fifth questions in the middle of the page may produce invalid responses while the other questions and the survey example are acceptable methods.**

The five typical questions that might be asked in a direct-response survey on page 5 are:
* What is the likelihood you would buy this product at a price of $25?
* At what price would you definitely buy this product?
* How much would you be willing to pay for this product?
* How much of this product would you buy at a price of 99¢?
* At which price difference would you switch from product A to product B?

The first and fourth questions are valid because they present a specific price and ask for a reaction (intention to buy or quantity to buy). In contrast, the second, third, and fifth questions are problematic because:

*   **"At what price would you definitely buy this product?"** This question directly asks for a price, which can lead to artificially low responses as consumers try to bargain down the price. It can also lead to highly variable and unreliable answers since it is difficult for people to think about their maximum willingness to pay in isolation from comparison shopping.
*   **"How much would you be willing to pay for this product?"** Similar to the second question, this question asks consumers to state their maximum willingness to pay which can lead to low responses. Furthermore, because it is an open-ended question, it is subject to interpretation by the respondents. The term "willing to pay" may cause consumers to think about what they wish they could pay (versus what they would actually pay), which is further affected by the context and their understanding of their financial means.
*   **"At which price difference would you switch from product A to product B?"** This question asks about a price *difference* rather than an absolute price point. This is problematic because the reference price is not standardized, making it difficult to compare across respondents. It also introduces the complexity of two product comparisons simultaneously, making it hard to isolate the value of just one product.

The survey method using purchase intentions on a scale with different price points (as in Table B) is valid because it:

*   **Provides context:** It gives a specific price and asks consumers to react to it on an intention-to-buy scale, rather than asking them to name a price.
*   **Avoids direct price elicitation:** It does not directly ask for a maximum willingness to pay, reducing the tendency for consumers to understate their true willingness to pay.
*   **Allows for comparison:** It uses multiple price points across different groups, allowing for better comparison of purchase intentions at varying prices.

**d. What factors might affect various customers’ sensitivity to a product’s price? How might such factors influence a company’s selection of a target market segment for its product?**

Factors that affect price sensitivity include:
*   **Magnitude of price:** Price sensitivity is higher for high-cost product categories than for low-cost ones.
    *   As the absolute dollar cost of the product increases.
    *   As the aggregate cost of ongoing usage increases.
    *   As the cost of that product increases as a percentage of the total cost.
*   **Who pays:** Price sensitivity is higher when the user is also the payer.
*   **Competitive factors:** Price sensitivity is higher when:
    *   Customers do not perceive significant differences in alternative products.
    *   It is easy to compare products and prices.
    *   It is easy to switch products.

These factors can influence a company's target market selection by identifying segments that are less price sensitive or more sensitive, and tailoring the marketing mix accordingly. For example, a company may target a segment less sensitive to price if it offers a differentiated product or if the target consumers are the ones who are paying the costs.

**e. What factors might influence the customization of product pricing across various market segments? Explain why a product may be priced differently for different market segments.**

Factors influencing price customization include:
*   **Taste:** Some customers may have a higher preference for a product than others.
*   **Importance of performance:** Customers with a higher need for performance may be willing to pay more.
*  **Ability to pay:** Customers with greater resources may be able to pay more.
*   **Intensity of use:** Those who use the product more frequently may value it more.
*   **Category knowledge:** Experienced customers may have a different understanding of value and prices.

A product may be priced differently across segments to capture variations in value, charging higher prices to those who value the product more. This can be achieved by:

*   **Product line differentiation:** Offering a range of products with different features at different prices.
*   **Controlling availability:** Offering different prices in different channels.
*   **Demographics:** Using demographic factors to provide different prices, for example, student, or senior discounts.
*  **Transaction characteristics:** Varying prices based on when or how the transaction takes place, such as early bird discounts.

**f. Describe alternative strategic marketing decisions that may influence a product’s pricing.**

Strategic marketing decisions that can influence pricing include:
*   **Low marketing expenditure/low price:** A strategy where the product's value speaks for itself and a low price makes it easier for consumers to buy.
*   **High marketing expenditure/high price:** A strategy where marketing is used to increase perceived value and the product is priced to capture that value. The higher price provides the margins necessary to fund the high marketing effort.

The article notes that high price/low marketing is not feasible in the long-term, and low price/high marketing results in no profits.

**g. What legal and ethical issues should be considered with respect to product pricing?**

Legal and ethical issues with product pricing include:
*   **Price customization** may be seen as unfair, particularly if it results in higher prices for certain groups, such as the poor or those with limited access to alternative sources.
*   **Anticompetitive pricing actions** such as:
    *   **Predatory pricing**: Pricing low for a time to drive out a competitor.
    *   **Price fixing**: Setting prices in collusion with competitors.
    *   **Price maintenance**: Requiring that distributors or retailers sell only at a specified price.
It is important to ensure pricing practices are ethical and that pricing does not illegally reduce competition.
