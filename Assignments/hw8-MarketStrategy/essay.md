## Marketing Strategy

This document provides an introduction to key marketing concepts crucial for entrepreneurs. As you read, consider the following study questions:

1. What are the two major activities of marketing strategy?
2. What are the elements of, and what insights can we gain from, the 5 C's marketing analysis?
3. What are the components of the 4 P's marketing mix, and how is each element determined?
4. Consider the cases of Vermeer and Palm—how was marketing strategy applied in each?

### Answers

#### 1. What are the two major activities of marketing strategy?

Marketing strategy consists of two primary activities:
1. **Selecting a target market and positioning the product** in the minds of the target customers.
2. **Planning and executing marketing activities** to achieve the desired positioning.

#### 2. What are the elements of the 5 C’s marketing analysis, and what do they reveal?

The 5 C’s of marketing analysis are:

- **Customers**: Identifying customer needs and understanding what problems the product or service aims to solve.
- **Company**: Assessing the firm’s unique skills, resources, and competitive advantages.
- **Competition**: Analyzing market competitors, their strengths, and weaknesses to differentiate effectively.
- **Collaborators**: Identifying key partners such as suppliers, distributors, and strategic allies.
- **Context**: Understanding broader environmental factors, including technological, economic, and legal trends.

This analysis forms the foundation for marketing decisions, including market selection, positioning, and marketing mix strategies.

#### 3. What are the elements of the 4 P’s marketing mix, and how is each element determined?

The 4 P’s of marketing include:

- **Product**: Encompasses the physical product, branding, post-sale support, and overall value proposition.
- **Place (Distribution Channels)**: Determines how a product reaches consumers, whether through direct, indirect, or hybrid distribution strategies.
- **Promotion**: Involves advertising, sales strategies, and communication efforts to build awareness and drive sales.
- **Price**: Reflects perceived value, competitive positioning, and pricing strategies like skimming or penetration pricing.

The configuration of the 4 P’s depends on the target market, competitive landscape, and customer expectations.

## Q2: Principles of Pricing

Consider the following questions as you read:

1. What do "objective value" and "perceived value" mean, and how do they relate to pricing?
2. What incentives drive consumer purchases and company production decisions?
3. How do pricing survey questions affect data validity?
4. What factors influence customer price sensitivity, and how do they shape market segmentation?
5. What factors justify pricing customization across market segments?
6. How do strategic marketing decisions impact pricing?
7. What legal and ethical issues should be considered in pricing decisions?

### Answers

#### 1. What is meant by “objective value” and “perceived value,” and how do they relate to pricing?

- **Objective value (True Economic Value - TEV)**: The inherent worth of a product based on its ability to meet customer needs, measured against alternatives.
- **Perceived value**: The value a consumer believes the product delivers, shaped by marketing, branding, and personal preferences.

A company’s pricing should fall between its cost of production and the customer’s perceived value to balance profitability and demand.

#### 2. What incentives drive consumer purchases and company production decisions?

- **Consumer incentive**: The difference between perceived value and price—if the perceived value exceeds the price, the consumer is motivated to buy.
- **Company incentive**: The difference between price and production cost—if the price covers costs and generates profit, the company is motivated to produce and sell.

#### 3. How do pricing survey questions affect data validity?

Certain pricing survey questions produce unreliable data. For instance:

- Asking respondents to name their “maximum willingness to pay” often results in artificially low responses.
- Asking about price differentials rather than absolute price points complicates interpretation.

A better approach involves using structured survey methods that test purchase intent at various price points.

#### 4. What factors influence customer price sensitivity, and how do they shape market segmentation?

Price sensitivity is influenced by:
- **Purchase context**: Higher sensitivity in essential, high-cost products.
- **Who pays**: Sensitivity increases when the buyer and user are the same person.
- **Competitive landscape**: Greater price sensitivity in markets with abundant alternatives.

Companies target less price-sensitive segments to enhance profitability through differentiation.

#### 5. What factors justify pricing customization across market segments?

Factors influencing price customization include:

- **Customer preferences**: Certain buyers value product features more than others.
- **Usage intensity**: Heavy users may derive more value and accept higher prices.
- **Ability to pay**: Wealthier customers or businesses often tolerate higher prices.
- **Market conditions**: Geographic and demographic variations impact pricing feasibility.

Pricing strategies include tiered product offerings, selective availability, and targeted discounts.

#### 6. How do strategic marketing decisions impact pricing?

Pricing strategies are influenced by:
- **High-price, high-marketing investment**: A premium pricing approach justified by brand positioning.
- **Low-price, low-marketing investment**: A cost-leadership strategy where affordability drives sales.

A misalignment between pricing and marketing investment can lead to profitability issues.

#### 7. What legal and ethical issues should be considered in pricing decisions?

Ethical and legal concerns include:
- **Price discrimination**: Charging different prices to different customers may be perceived as unfair.
- **Predatory pricing**: Temporarily lowering prices to eliminate competition can be illegal.
- **Price fixing**: Coordinating prices with competitors is prohibited.

Companies must balance profitability with fairness and compliance with pricing regulations.
