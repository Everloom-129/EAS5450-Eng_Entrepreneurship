## Growing Up But Not Growing Old



N12 Technologies scaled in three clear phases: from 4 to 7, 7 to 16, and finally to 27 employees. In each phase, it navigated the tension between structure and agility with surprising maturity for a startup rooted in advanced R&D.



The first stage, from 4 to 7, was flat and fluid. Roles weren't clearly defined, but the horizontal expansion brought in key expertise without hierarchy. Everyone contributed broadly—scientists, engineers, and even interns shared equal voice. This helped validate NanoStitch and build credibility with early customers.



The next phase, scaling to 16, required vertical structure. Teams formed under Williams, Degtiarov, and Gouldstone, each focusing separately on production, engineering, and applications. Actually, team's shift from "doing" to "managing." Communication challenges emerged, but COO Trip Flavin brought discipline—introducing daily check-ins, weekly goals, and rhythm to operations. It was a necessary pivot from intuition to intentionality.



At 27 scale, coordination became harder, but N12 actively preserved its startup spirit. For example, the regular informal breakfast meetings built trust among early members. While formal systems—performance reviews, team meetings, shared dashboards—helped communication for standard and efficient. 



All in all, N12 stayed young by fostering humility and trust. Founders complemented each other; early engineers adapted to manage teams without ego. Input was welcomed across levels. In sum, N12 didn't just scale—it matured intentionally, preserving its core identity while building the foundation for future growth.
