Here's a summary of the initial public offering (IPO) process, including factors that impact a company's decision to go public, the role of underwriters, and the alignment of incentives in pricing the offering, according to the sources: 

 **Internal and External Factors Impacting the IPO Decision** 

 *   **Need to raise capital** Many companies, especially in technology and biotechnology, need significant capital to introduce new products, which may be difficult to obtain from other sources like banks or venture capital. Follow-on offerings may be easier to arrange after an IPO. 
 *   **Desire to achieve liquidity** Entrepreneurs often seek diversification by selling shares, and private equity investors want to liquidate investments to achieve returns. However, insiders are typically restricted from selling shares at the time of the IPO due to concerns that it may deter new investors. 
 *   **Interactions with customers and suppliers** Public status can project stability and dependability, which is particularly important for ongoing service or upgrades. Enhanced visibility is also a key reason for foreign companies to go public on the NASDAQ. 
 *   **Market conditions** The market's appetite for new issues varies over time, with IPO volume changing dramatically from year to year, often following periods of rising stock prices. 

 **Potential Disadvantages** 

 *   **Substantial fees** Legal, accounting, and investment banking fees can be very high, sometimes 10% or more of the total capital raised. 
 *   **Increased scrutiny** Public companies face a high degree of disclosure and scrutiny, which can be troubling, especially for family businesses. 
 *   **Risk of a withdrawn offering** A company may be "tainted" if it files to go public but subsequently withdraws the offering. 

 **Role of the Underwriter** 

 *   **Selecting an underwriter** Firms evaluate banks based on the reputation of their research analysts, commitments to provide coverage, and past IPO performance. 
 *   **Multiple underwriters** Firms may select multiple underwriters, including specialized and larger banks, but only one will be the lead, or book, underwriter. 
 *   **Due diligence and marketing** The underwriter conducts due diligence, determines the offering size, prepares marketing materials, and assists with regulatory filings. 
 *   **Pricing and stabilization** Underwriters gather information on demand to set the price, factor in valuations of comparable firms, and use discounted cash-flow analyses. They also commit to stabilizing the price after the offering, often using the "Green Shoe" option to sell additional shares or buy them back to support the price. 

 **Incentives and Influence in Pricing** 

 *   **Alignment of incentives** Underwriters want a successful IPO that raises capital and sets the stage for future offerings. Companies want the share price to rise. 
 *   **Underwriter's influence** Investment banks may deliberately set offering prices too low to transfer wealth to select investors, who may then reward the bank with other transactions. 
 *   **Conflicts of interest** Underwriters manage potential conflicts by building relationships with companies and offering equity analysis. They earn fees by selling stock to customers who want to buy shares at a low price.

Here's an analysis of the Facebook IPO case, addressing the study questions based on the provided sources: 

 **a. How does Facebook make money? What are the value drivers of its business? What is its comparative advantage relative to other social networking companies?** 

 *   **Revenue sources**: Facebook primarily generated revenue through **advertising, which accounted for 85% of its revenue in 2011**. The balance came from its payments business, mainly from virtual goods used in social games like Zynga. 
 *   **Value drivers**: Facebook's value was driven by its ability to **connect users and provide a platform for sharing content**. Key metrics included: 
 *   **Monthly Active Users (MAUs)**: Totaled 845 million by year-end 2011. 
 *   **Daily Active Users (DAUs)**:  483 million in December 2011. 
 *   **Average Revenue Per User (ARPU)**:  $5.11 in 2011. 
 *   **Comparative advantage**: Facebook's main advantage was its **extensive, proprietary database** that mapped connections between users and their friends, and recorded products or services they had "liked". This allowed advertisers to target customized services and products based on user preferences and connections. Facebook required users to disclose their authentic identity online and any information uploaded became the property of the firm. 

 **b. Why is Facebook going public? What is the planned use of proceeds from the offering?** 

 *   **Reasons for going public**: The primary goals of the IPO were to **create a public market for existing shareholders** and to **enable future access to public equity markets**. 
 *   **Use of proceeds**: The proceeds from the IPO were intended for **working capital and other general corporate purposes**. 

 **c. What was going on in the U.S. IPO markets prior to Facebook’s offering? What has been the performance of recent IPOs?** 

 *   **Market conditions**: The IPO was happening during a fragile global economic environment, with the U.S. economy recovering slowly from the 2007-2009 financial crisis. Market volatility and economic uncertainty had left global IPO markets in the doldrums. 
 *   **Recent IPO performance**:  Recent tech IPOs showed mixed results: 
 *   **LinkedIn**:  Priced at $45, the stock rose 109% on its first day and continued to increase. 
 *   **Groupon**: Priced above its initial range at $20, shares rose 43% on the first day but later fell. 
 *   **Zynga**: Priced at $10, the share price fell on the first day and continued to trade below the IPO price. 

 **d. What is the intrinsic value of a Facebook share? How does this valuation compare to the price talk from the underwriters?** 

 *   **Valuation**: The initial price range was in the high $20s to mid-$30s, later increased to $34 to $38 per share. This valued Facebook at over $100 billion. 
 *   **Valuation concerns**: The valuation seemed expensive, with a multiple of nearly 100 times trailing 12-month earnings and 26 times trailing 12-month sales. 
 *   **Damodaran's DCF**: Aswath Damodaran from NYU Stern School of Business estimated a value based on a discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis. He noted that at a $75 billion valuation, the market expected Facebook to become a phenomenal success. 

 **e. Does the case raise any red flags that could foresee the problems reported in the article “Facebook’s IPO Sputters”?** 

 *   **High valuation**: The high valuation, even by technology standards, could be a red flag. 
 *   **Dual-class share structure**:  Mark Zuckerberg’s control, through Class B shares, over 56% of the voting power was a concern. This meant he could control the outcome of matters submitted to Facebook’s stockholders for approval. 
 *   **Sales by current shareholders**: A significant number of shares were being sold by existing stockholders, which could be a concern. 
 *   **Mobile monetization**: Facebook's inability to display ads to mobile users at the time of the IPO threatened to cannibalize online advertising revenues. 

 **f. As a potential shareholder, what are your concerns about Facebook or its stock offering? Would you invest in this initial public offering of Facebook shares?** 

 *   **Concerns**: 
 *   **Valuation**: Overpaying for the stock was a risk. 
 *   **Control**: Zuckerberg’s concentrated control meant other shareholders would not have meaningful input into Facebook’s strategic choices. 
 *   **Lock-up periods**:  The lock-up periods meant a large number of shares would become available for sale in the near future, potentially causing the stock price to decline. 
 *   **Competition**: The social networking space was competitive, with companies like MySpace losing their lead quickly. 
 *   **Dependence on advertising**: Heavy reliance on advertising revenue could be a risk. 
 *   **Mobile transition:** Inability to monetize mobile users. 
 *   **Investment decision**: Given the information, an investor would need to weigh the potential for Facebook to deliver above-average returns against the risks of overpaying and the concerns about governance and market conditions.
