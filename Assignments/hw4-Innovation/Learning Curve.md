Here is a breakdown of the study questions related to the Sales Learning Curve, based on the provided sources:

**a. Why does the article emphasize that a large sales force hinders more than helps a company launch a new product?**

- According to the article, a large sales force can hinder a new product launch because most new products aren't fully developed with the features customers want or don't function as they should.
- Adding sales capacity too early in the launch prevents a company from addressing challenges such as identifying and resolving service issues and developing a repeatable sales model.
- Hiring a full sales force too quickly leads a company to burn through cash and fail to meet revenue expectations because the organization needs time to learn how customers will acquire and use the product.
- A large sales staff hinders the company from learning from its customers and responding to them, which is necessary for modifying both the product and the processes associated with making and selling it.
- Conventional sales wisdom suggests that the only way to drive sales is by hiring sales reps as early as possible. However, this approach does not take into account the organization's sales learning curve and can result in a company being overwhelmed and wasting resources.
- The company needs to focus first on learning from customers rather than focusing on rapidly acquiring customers with a large sales force.

**b. What is a “sales learning curve?” How is it different from a “manufacturing learning curve?”**

- The sales learning curve is the process by which a company learns how customers will acquire and use a new product, and then modifies the product and sales tactics based on what it learns. This is a give-and-take between the company and its customers.
- The manufacturing learning curve involves transferring knowledge and experience between a production line and various departments, improving the efficiency and lowering the cost of the process over time.
- The sales learning curve involves all customer-facing parts of the organization (marketing, sales, product support, and product development) whereas the manufacturing learning curve involves the production and related departments.
- The sales learning curve is about learning from customers how to sell the product, and how they use it; the manufacturing learning curve is focused on improving production efficiency.
- Progress along the sales learning curve is measured by sales yield, defined as average annual sales revenue per full-time, fully trained, and effective sales representative. The manufacturing learning curve is measured by tracking costs per unit.

**c. Describe the three phases of the sales learning curve.**

- **Initiation Phase:** This is the first phase after beta testing the product where the focus is on learning how customers will use the product and supporting the company in refining the offering as well as marketing and selling strategies. The goal is to reach the break-even point where revenue per sales rep equals the fully loaded cost per sales rep.
- **Transition Phase:** In this phase, a company has acquired a critical mass of customers, and sales are accelerating. The initial sales team remains focused on learning while new sales reps are added who can operate within an evolving sales model. The goal is to reach a point where the product has achieved real traction in the market, typically when sales yield is twice the fully loaded cost per sales rep.
- **Execution Phase:** In this phase, the company has developed a formula for success and the sales force's support requirements are in place. Traditional salespeople are hired and armed with the tools needed to take orders. The goal is to scale sales rapidly as the company moves from break-even to profitability.

**d. How does the interaction with your customers differ in the “initiation phase” from the subsequent “transition” and “execution” phases? What personal qualities do your salespeople need in this initial phase as compared to the subsequent phases?**

- In the **initiation phase**, salespeople need to focus on learning from customers and supporting other departments in refining the product. They need to be able to communicate well with other teams, tolerate ambiguity, have a deep interest in the product technology, bring customers together with various functional teams, and develop their own sales models and collateral materials. These individuals are described as "renaissance reps".
- In the **transition phase**, the initial team continues to focus on learning, while new sales reps are added who can operate effectively within an evolving sales model but don’t need the same analytical and communication skills as the initial team. These individuals are described as "enlightened reps".
- In the **execution phase**, traditional salespeople are hired who require a territory, a sales plan, a price book, and marketing materials to bring in orders. These individuals are described as "coin-operated reps".

**e. Describe the dangers of a premature marketing campaign.**

- A premature marketing campaign is an expensive use of resources and can distract the organization from its primary learning goal.
- It can also set false expectations among the sales and engineering groups.
- It may set false expectations in the marketplace that will be difficult to correct.
- The product and positioning evolve in the first two phases of the sales learning curve, so marketing communications must be nimble and adjust to each stage, with easily modified collateral materials.

**f. The article discusses interactions between a company’s sales force, marketing department, engineering department and senior management. How does this translate to a start-up company where the entrepreneurial team is unlikely to be so departmentalized?**

- In a startup, the entrepreneurial team is unlikely to be departmentalized, but they must still fulfill the roles of sales, marketing, engineering, and senior management.
- The entrepreneurial team must focus on the customer frontier, the source of learning, and work collaboratively to refine the product and sales strategy.
- The team needs to take responsibility for projecting the shape of the sales learning curve, ensuring learning opportunities are identified, and setting realistic expectations.
- The team must work together to make decisions about product functionality, target markets, sales channels, and marketing strategy.
- The founders should be the first salespeople, using their passion and knowledge to understand customer needs.
- One founder may need to dedicate their time to sales, learning and refining the sales process, as Tyler Bosmeny did at Clever.

**g. How are the sales learning curve and innovation funnel (from the reading above) related? How are they different?**

- The **innovation funnel** is a framework for thinking about the product development process, which starts with a wide range of ideas and concepts, and gradually narrows down to a specific product. The funnel represents a reduction of options as the project evolves. The Stage-Gate model complements the funnel by providing a structure for making go/no-go decisions throughout the product development process.
- The **sales learning curve** is a framework for understanding how to effectively sell a new product, focusing on the iterative learning process between the company and its customers. It explains the relationship between the company's learning, sales force size and skills, and the different stages of product launch.
- Both frameworks emphasize the importance of learning, but the innovation funnel is focused on product development whereas the sales learning curve is focused on how to sell the product.
- The innovation funnel and stage-gate model are used to manage a portfolio of projects, while the sales learning curve is used to manage the launch of a single new product.
- The sales learning curve begins after a product has been beta tested and is ready to hit the market, which corresponds to the later stages of the innovation funnel or the stage gate model.
- The sales learning curve can inform decisions made within the later phases of the innovation funnel or stage-gate model, such as product design, target market selection, pricing, and sales strategy.

In summary, the sales learning curve emphasizes the importance of learning from customers, refining products and sales strategies, and scaling sales efforts gradually over time. It also highlights the risks associated with prematurely expanding a sales force or launching a marketing campaign before a product is fully developed and its market is understood.





###  "Innovation: A Customer-Driven Approach" reading:

- **Assessing Market Potential for a New, High-Tech, First-of-its-Kind Product:** Assessing the market potential for a new, high-tech, first-of-its-kind product involves a series of steps, which are often revised and refined throughout the development process. While the reading notes that these tasks are relatively straightforward, it also cautions that they are often inaccurate. Here are some of the approaches for assessing market demand:
  - **Population-based estimates**: Estimate the percentage of the total population, number of households, or companies that will buy in the product category each year. For a new product category, models predict that the number of units bought over time will resemble an S-shaped curve.
  - **Population \* usage-based estimates**: For products with short intervals between purchases, the market size is calculated by multiplying the number of customers by the number of purchase occasions per year, and the average number of units purchased on each occasion.
  - **Forecasting by analogy**: Examine the market size of similar products in their early years to create a baseline for the new product.
  - **Forecasting by percentage of complementary product sales**: Estimate the percentage of sales that a new product will achieve based on the sales of a complementary product.
  - **Forecasting based on past competitor performance**: Estimate a new entrant's market share based on the experience of competitors, taking into account their investments and first-year sales.
  - It's important to note that these forecasts should be challenged and companies should plan for contingencies. **Reducing cycle time in new product development, manufacturing, and delivery can allow companies to respond to the marketplace and deliver products that customers have already ordered in advance**.
- **Stakeholders and Their Role:** **Stakeholders are all groups that have a stake in the development, existence, and obsolescence of a product**. These can include, but are not limited to, groups within the company (e.g., manufacturing, design, shipping, purchasing), those who make buying decisions, the end users of the product, and even those who will dispose of the product. Each of these stakeholders plays a role in shaping an idea into a product.
  - Companies should not only seek input from "the customers" but also consider the needs of all stakeholders, although **not every group needs to be considered to the same degree**.
  - It's important to gather information from stakeholders through direct observation and personal interviews, ideally in the setting where the product will be used. This can uncover needs that stakeholders themselves may not be able to articulate.
  - When stakeholder needs conflict, **compromises can be made**.
  - The more empathetic the development team is towards the product's stakeholders, the more likely the company will introduce a satisfying product.
- **Concepts vs. Ideas:**
  - **Ideas** are the initial thoughts or inspirations for a potential product. These may come from an individual or a group.
  - **Concepts** are more concrete and describe how a product will meet stakeholder needs by describing, usually through a drawing or model and some general text, what technology and form the product will employ.
  - **Concepts evolve from ideas by translating customer needs into product features**.
  - The distinctive combination of the answers for the "who," "what," and "how" questions is a product concept's **positioning statement**, where Positioning = Differentiation (what + how) + Segmentation (for whom).
  - A product concept includes a description of the product's performance and technical features, how it should be configured, how it should serve customers, who those customers are, and its meaning to those customers (e.g., status, personality, feel, image).
  - Concepts should provide a common set of objectives for the entire development team to act upon.
- **Products vs. Concepts:**
  - **Concepts** are descriptions, drawings, or models of what a product should be, representing how the product will meet stakeholder needs.
  - **Products** are the tangible or intangible outputs of the development process that are brought to market.
  - **Products evolve from concepts through a process of design, engineering, and prototyping**.
  - The product must have internal and external integrity, where the product’s components fit together well and meet the needs of the customer.
- **Purposes of Prototypes:** **Prototypes are representations of all or a portion of a product** and they are created during the development process to serve a number of purposes. Prototypes are used to:
- **Test the feasibility of a concept**: This involves physical or analytical modeling to determine if the pieces fit together, if the product can be built at a low enough cost, and if the product will function as intended.
- **Collect customer/stakeholder feedback**: Prototypes can be used to get feedback on the product's feel, sturdiness, and other factors.
- **Communicate an idea**: Prototypes can be used to show how a product differs from a competitor to gain more investment or pursue one concept instead of another.
- It is important to determine how accurate and complete the prototypes should be, and who should be involved in their construction and testing.



### What are the three phases of the sales learning curve?

The sales learning curve is a framework for understanding how a company learns to sell a new product, and it unfolds in three distinct phases:

- **Initiation Phase:** This is the first phase and it begins when the product is ready to hit the market, having been beta tested.
  - During this phase, few customers are willing to buy, and those who do may require incentives.
  - It is important to focus on learning as much as possible about how customers use the product to refine both the product itself and the go-to-market strategy.
  - A small sales team of about three or four people is ideal, and those salespeople should have strong communication skills, a tolerance for ambiguity, a deep interest in product technology, and the ability to bring customers together with the company's functional teams. These individuals are often referred to as "renaissance reps" because they need to be resourceful and able to develop their own sales models and collateral materials.
  - It is not effective to assign large sales quotas or to hire too many salespeople in this phase, and compensation should not be heavily commission-based, because that can inhibit learning.
  - The initiation phase lasts until sales yield reaches the break-even point, where the revenue per sales rep equals the fully loaded cost per sales rep.
- **Transition Phase:** This phase begins once the company has acquired a critical mass of customers, and sales start to accelerate.
  - The original "renaissance reps" should continue to focus on learning.
  - New sales representatives can be added, but they should be comfortable working within an evolving sales model and do not need to have the analytical and communication skills of the original team. These new reps are often called “enlightened reps”.
  - During this phase, sales management focuses on developing a repeatable sales model and refining market positioning, while adding sales capacity at a rate that corresponds to the rise in the sales curve.
  - This phase continues until the sales yield reaches a point where the company can see that the product has achieved real traction in the market. The end of this stage is often marked by a sales yield of twice the fully loaded cost per sales representative.
- **Execution Phase:** Once the product has achieved traction and the formula for success has been developed, the company enters the execution phase.
  - At this point, the company can hire traditional salespeople, sometimes called "coin-operated reps", who require only a territory, sales plan, price book, and marketing materials to bring in orders.
  - Sales representatives can be hired as rapidly as management and financial constraints allow.

By understanding these three phases of the sales learning curve, companies can better manage their resources, set realistic expectations, and increase the chances of success when launching a new product.
