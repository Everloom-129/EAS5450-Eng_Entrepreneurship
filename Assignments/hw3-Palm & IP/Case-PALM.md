Jeff Hawkins, the founder of Palm Computing, faced a complex situation regarding the intellectual property protection of his pattern recognition algorithm, PalmPrint. While the patent provided certain advantages, it also presented some disadvantages, which he had to carefully weigh. According to the case study, Hawkins patented his PalmPrint algorithm because he believed that patents have real value and are an important asset in raising venture funds. Even though he knew the patent did not provide absolute protection against someone else using his algorithm, it was something he could sell to investors or license to other companies. As Hawkins stated, it was the "currency of intellectual property" that investors perceived as valuable. The patent also gave Palm a sense of credibility and legitimacy, as it was seen as having the "protection of the U.S. government behind it". From a strategic standpoint, this perception was crucial for Hawkins as he was trying to establish his new venture as an independent entity and attract potential investors. Moreover, it provided a basis for his negotiations with Tandy. Hawkins also knew that he could license the technology to other companies. This could potentially bring in additional revenue streams for Palm, and it gave Hawkins some additional leverage in his negotiations with Tandy.

However, the patenting process also presented some drawbacks. As part of obtaining a patent, Hawkins was required to disclose and publish the algorithm in its entirety. This meant that anyone could read and potentially copy it. Hawkins was aware that the algorithm, being a basic building block, could be buried within an application, making it difficult to detect. This inherent vulnerability of the patented algorithm meant that while he could obtain the patent, he could not be sure it would actually prevent others from using it. Moreover, the process of obtaining a patent was more difficult and time-consuming than securing a copyright, but he believed the benefit outweighed the cost. He also had to consider the restrictions imposed by his licensing agreement with GRiD. The agreement granted GRiD rights to use PalmPrint with its hardware computing systems in exchange for royalty payments. This created a non-exclusive, but restrictive agreement where Hawkins could not license the technology to any of GRiD's competitors. Also, any enhancements made to PalmPrint after Hawkins rejoined GRiD were the property of the corporation. This limited his ability to utilize improvements made while at GRiD for his own company, which were critical to the success of his new venture, as well as impacting his negotiations with Tandy. The patent also had limitations in its ability to provide complete protection against misuse as highlighted by the GRiD’s experiences with a patent they needed to acquire.

Furthermore, Hawkins had to think about the patent's implications on his relationship with Tandy. While he owned the PalmPrint patent, Tandy held the rights to all product developments made by employees, including Hawkins, since GRiD acquired the license in 1988. He was aware that he could not use the license as a bargaining chip with Tandy, because Tandy had the right to use PalmPrint even if Hawkins left. This meant that while Hawkins owned the patent, he had to rely on negotiating with Tandy in order to use the improvements that had been made to his original work. This created a critical need for Tandy’s cooperation, making it a key challenge in the launch of Palm Computing. Hawkins understood that his professional reputation could open doors to resources, but the PalmPrint patent was still a significant asset that had both financial and technological value, because it could be licensed or sold.

In conclusion, Hawkins' decision to patent PalmPrint was a strategic move aimed at raising capital and establishing legitimacy for his new venture. While the patent did not offer complete protection against misuse, its perceived value made it a valuable asset for negotiations and attracting investors. However, this value needed to be balanced against the disadvantages such as public disclosure of the algorithm, the limitations of the licensing agreement with GRiD, and the need for additional agreements with Tandy. Overall, his intellectual property protection provided advantages that were useful, and also posed some disadvantages that needed to be navigated as part of launching his new venture.
