# Lec 20: Guest Speaker — Tobias Dengel, CEO of WillowTree (acq. TELUS Digital, $1.2B)

*April 2025*

> WillowTree went from a two-person mobile app shop in 2008 to 1,000+ people and a $1.2 billion acquisition in 2023. The through-line: pick the right technology wave, build the right culture, and never stop selling.

## The Big Picture

Tobias Dengel co-founded WillowTree Apps in 2008 — right at the dawn of the iPhone App Store era. The company grew into a world-class digital product consultancy serving Fortune 500 clients across mobile apps, voice technology, and AI-powered digital experiences. Its acquisition by TELUS Digital in 2023 for $1.2B is a remarkable outcome for a bootstrapped services company.

Tobias is also the author of *The Sound of the Future* — a Wall Street Journal bestseller on voice technology — which gives him a rare dual perspective: practitioner and theorist.

---

## 3 Facts That Hit Different

- **WillowTree bootstrapped for its entire existence** before the TELUS acquisition — no VC funding. This is exceptional for a company that reached $1B+ valuation. The services model (revenue from client work from day one) made bootstrapping viable; the discipline it required shaped the company's culture around sustainable growth over hypergrowth.

- **TELUS Digital's client roster** includes Google, Meta, Airbnb, Capital One, T-Mobile, Marriott, Domino's, and dozens of Fortune 500 companies. WillowTree built this client base through consistent delivery quality and deep long-term relationships — not through aggressive sales or marketing spend.

- **Voice technology is still in its first innings.** Tobias's book argues that the combination of LLMs and voice interfaces will fundamentally change how humans interact with technology — from touchscreens back to natural speech, but now augmented by genuine AI comprehension. Companies that understand this shift now will have a significant advantage in the next decade.

## Q&A

**Q: What lessons from WillowTree apply to a technical founder building a company in 2025?**

Tobias distilled it to six principles in the lecture:

1. **Be yourself** — you can be inspired by Elon Musk but you can't *be* Elon Musk. Know what you're uniquely good at and build from that.
2. **Business is driven by sales** — engineering skill is table stakes; the differentiator is being able to sell.
3. **Sales is driven by marketing** — brand, narrative, and positioning determine who finds you before the sales conversation begins.
4. **Culture is everything** — especially in a services business, culture IS the product. The people who show up to your client's problem *are* your offering.
5. **Make the trend your friend** — timing is leverage. WillowTree launched with mobile; voice/AI is the next wave.
6. **Technology always takes over jobs** — this is a statement of opportunity, not anxiety. Every automation wave creates new categories of work; founders win by being early to the new category.

**Q: How do you preserve entrepreneurial agility inside a large corporate structure (TELUS)?**

The honest answer: with difficulty and intentionality. Large organizations optimize for consistency and risk reduction; entrepreneurial units need speed and risk tolerance. Tobias described this as a constant negotiation — fighting for the autonomy to move fast while operating within the accountability structures of a $9B company.

The strategies that work: **autonomous P&L units** (your team owns its own revenue and cost), **clear cultural artifacts** (values and behaviors that persist through organizational change), and **leadership who understands what you're protecting and why**. Without executive-level sponsorship for your differentiated culture, it gets averaged out.

**Q: How is generative AI changing digital product development, and how should founders think about this shift?**

This is the question that's restructuring the entire industry Tobias operates in. His framing: AI doesn't eliminate the need for digital products — it changes *what* those products are and *how* they're built. A product that would have taken 18 months to build in 2022 can be prototyped in 6 weeks today. This compresses timelines but also raises the bar for what "differentiated" means.

For founders: the opportunity is not to build AI tools — thousands of companies are doing that. The opportunity is to use AI to build *domain-specific products* that solve real problems for real industries, faster and cheaper than was previously possible. The competitive advantage is the domain knowledge, not the AI itself.

## My Take

WillowTree's story challenges a narrative I've absorbed from startup culture: that B2C, VC-backed, hypergrowth is the only credible path. WillowTree bootstrapped, grew steadily, served large enterprises, and sold for $1.2B. That's a legitimate and in many ways *superior* outcome — lower risk, more control, no dilution, predictable growth.

Tobias's "make the trend your friend" principle is one I keep coming back to. The trends I'm betting on: embodied AI, physical automation, and human-robot collaboration. Getting to market in these areas *now*, before the market is mature, is the leverage point. The same way WillowTree's early mobile expertise created 15 years of compounding advantage, early robotics expertise today could create a similar runway.
