# Lec 8: Market Strategy — The 5 C's, 4 P's, and the Price That Gets Paid

*February 12, 2025*

> Price is not a number. It's a signal. Set it too low and you signal low quality. Set it too high and you signal arrogance. The art is in calibrating the signal to your target customer's reference points.

## The Big Picture

Marketing strategy is two things: **who you sell to** (target market + positioning) and **how you sell to them** (the marketing mix). Most engineering founders skip directly to tactics — ads, growth hacks, press coverage — without first anchoring on these two foundational questions.

The 5 C's framework (Customers, Company, Competition, Collaborators, Context) is the analytical layer. The 4 P's (Product, Place, Promotion, Price) are the execution layer. You need both, and in that order.

---

## 3 Facts That Hit Different

- **Pricing surveys that ask "what's the most you'd pay?" consistently produce artificially low responses.** Customers anchor low to protect themselves. Better survey method: test purchase intent at multiple price points and watch conversion behavior. Revealed preference beats stated preference.

- **Perceived value ≠ objective value.** A product worth $200 in engineering hours may be perceived as worth $500 by one customer segment and $50 by another. Pricing to cost is a mistake; pricing to perceived value in your target segment is how high-tech companies generate margin.

- **Predatory pricing** — temporarily cutting prices to eliminate a competitor — is illegal under antitrust law in most jurisdictions. "Price fixing" (coordinating prices with competitors) is also prohibited. These constraints are real and frequently underestimated by founders who think pricing is purely a competitive tool.

## Q&A

**Q: What are the 5 C's and why do they have to come before the 4 P's?**

The 5 C's are your situational analysis:

- **Customers**: What problems do they have? What do they value?
- **Company**: What are your actual strengths and constraints?
- **Competition**: Who else is solving this problem, and how?
- **Collaborators**: Who are the partners, distributors, suppliers you depend on?
- **Context**: What macro forces (regulatory, technological, economic) shape your environment?

Without this analysis, the 4 P's are just guesses. You might price a product at $99 because it feels right — but if your key competitor is at $49 and customers perceive them as equivalent, you've priced yourself out of the market without knowing why.

**Q: What is "True Economic Value" and how should it drive pricing?**

True Economic Value (TEV) = (price of the best alternative) + (value of the performance differential). It measures what your product is *actually worth* to a customer compared to doing nothing or using the next-best option.

Pricing strategy should sit between your cost (floor) and TEV (ceiling). The closer you can price to TEV, the higher your margin. But this requires understanding customer perception — because TEV varies by segment. Enterprise buyers may perceive your SaaS product's TEV as $50,000/year; SMBs may perceive it as $500/year. Same product, completely different pricing potential.

**Q: How did Vermeer and Palm approach the 4 P's differently?**

**Vermeer (FrontPage):**
- *Product*: A complete web authoring tool, not just a text editor. WYSIWYG was the differentiator.
- *Place*: Initially direct sales to technical professionals; post-Microsoft acquisition, mass distribution.
- *Promotion*: PR-heavy, leveraging Ferguson's network and media appearances. $300K budget forced creative tactics.
- *Price*: Targeted at professional users who valued ease of use enough to pay a premium.

**Palm (PalmPilot):**
- *Product*: Deliberately minimal feature set — contact, calendar, notes, sync. Not a PC replacement.
- *Place*: Initially targeted through tech-savvy retail channels; scaled after U.S. Robotics acquisition.
- *Promotion*: Direct demonstrations to industry leaders and press. The product sold itself once people held it.
- *Price*: Positioned as affordable ($299) relative to competitors — accessible without being cheap.

Both succeeded by tight alignment between their target segment and every element of the marketing mix. Misalignment in any one P undermines the others.

## My Take

The pricing lecture is where entrepreneurship gets the most uncomfortable for engineers. We're trained to think cost-plus: materials + labor + margin = price. But customers don't care about your cost structure. They care about what the product does for them relative to alternatives.

The perceived value insight shows up everywhere in hardware and software: brand signal matters as much as the technical spec. Two products with near-identical specs can sell at 3–5x different price points if one has a better support reputation or a more trusted brand. That's TEV thinking in practice — buyers aren't pricing your cost structure, they're pricing their own risk and the alternatives available to them.

The 5 C's analysis is something I'll do for any venture I consider. Context especially: in robotics, the regulatory environment (FAA, FDA, DOT) is a non-obvious constraint that changes the market structure entirely. Ignoring context doesn't make it go away.
