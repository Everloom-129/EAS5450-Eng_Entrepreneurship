## The Big Picture

Why does Penn Engineering have such an entrepreneurship course? program the world is full of brilliant technology that never reaches people who need it, not because the science was wrong, but because nobody figured out how to build a company around it.

EAS5450 Engineering Entrepreneurship is built on a simple premise: **technical expertise is necessary but not sufficient**. The engineers who have the greatest impact on the world are the ones who understand markets, customers, financing, strategy, and leadership well enough to bring their technology to scale. This course is the bridge.

Over 27+ lectures, we'll study real companies: HP, Palm, Vermeer, Dropbox, LinkedIn, Facebook, GenapSys, Theranos, N12 Technologies, and more... Then we extract the frameworks that explain why some succeed and others fail. We'll hear from real founders. We'll wrestle with real dilemmas.

## 3 Facts That Hit Different

- **95% of venture-backed startups fail to return capital** to investors. This isn't a bug, it's the structure of a system where a few massive wins compensate for many losses. But for founders, the failure rate makes every decision higher-stakes. Understanding why companies fail is the first step to not failing.

- **The median time from founding to IPO for a venture-backed technology company is 8–10 years.** This is a long game. The frameworks in this course are not quick fixes, they're thinking tools for decisions you'll be making over a decade.

- **The United States produces more engineering PhDs than any other country**, yet consistently imports entrepreneurial talent from abroad to build its most important companies. The bottleneck isn't technical talent — it's the willingness and ability to commercialize. This course addresses the bottleneck.

## Q&A

**Q: What is "engineering entrepreneurship" and how is it different from just "entrepreneurship"?**

Entrepreneurship is the general skill of building a venture under uncertainty — identifying opportunities, assembling resources, executing against a vision. Engineering entrepreneurship is a specific variant: you start with a technology insight or capability and figure out how to build a business around it.

The challenges are different. A consumer-facing startup can often test its hypotheses quickly through A/B tests and user interviews. A deep-tech startup may need 3–5 years of R&D before you have something testable at all. The customer discovery process, the funding path, the go-to-market — all are shaped by the technology constraints.

Engineering entrepreneurs also have a specific cognitive bias to unwatch: the tendency to believe that a technically superior product will win on its own. It won't. Palm was technically superior to Newton. Betamax was technically superior to VHS. Superior technology is a necessary but not sufficient condition for market success.

**Q: What is the "Case Method" and why does this course use it?**

The Case Method — developed at Harvard Business School — is a pedagogy built around analyzing real company decisions under conditions of incomplete information. You read the case, put yourself in the decision-maker's shoes, and argue for what should be done.

The point is not to find the "right answer" — in most cases there isn't one. The point is to practice **structured reasoning under uncertainty**, which is exactly what entrepreneurship requires. Every case in this course is a training scenario for the judgment calls you'll face when running your own company.

**Q: What is the single most important mindset shift this course requires?**

Moving from **"what can I build?"** to **"what should I build, and for whom?"** Engineers are trained to optimize for technical elegance. Entrepreneurs have to optimize for value created for a specific customer. These are related but different objectives, and the habit of thinking customer-first is the most important skill this course develops.
