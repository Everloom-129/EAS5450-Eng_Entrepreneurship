# Tweet Thread — EAS5450 Retrospective

> Topic: How Engineering Entrepreneurship refactored my vision of AI + Robotics as a business model
> Platform: X (Twitter)
> Format: 8-tweet thread

---

**1/**
I took an Engineering Entrepreneurship course at Penn a year ago.

I thought it was about startups. It was actually about how to think.

Here's what changed how I see AI + Robotics as a business — a thread 🧵

---

**2/**
Most robotics engineers (including me) build things we want to exist.

That's a great starting point and a terrible ending point.

The gap between "this technology is impressive" and "enough people will pay for this to build a business" — that gap has a name. It's called the Chasm.

---

**3/**
Geoffrey Moore's insight: Early Adopters and Early Majority don't just have different needs — they psychologically mistrust each other.

Visionaries want revolution. Pragmatists want references.

Most robotics startups die in the gap between these two groups.

---

**4/**
The fix isn't a better robot. It's a more complete product.

Whole product = core tech + integration support + training + ecosystem + proof that your customer's peers already use it.

AI/Robotics companies fail not because the tech doesn't work — but because they ship the tech without shipping the solution.

---

**5/**
The hardest lesson: the status quo is your real competitor.

Switching to your product means: direct cost + learning curve + uncertainty + losing familiar tools + convincing the whole team.

Your robot has to be good enough to overcome ALL of that. Not just better on a spec sheet.

---

**6/**
Schumpeter's insight aged well: the entrepreneur isn't primarily a risk-taker.

They're a disruptor — driven by the joy of creating something that makes the current configuration of the world obsolete.

That maps exactly to what's happening in AI right now. The motivation isn't expected return. It's conviction that the current state is wrong.

---

**7/**
A year ago I saw AI + Robotics as a research problem.

Now I see it as a systems problem: technology × go-to-market × culture × timing.

The research is necessary but not sufficient. The business model is where the impact actually scales.

---

**8/**
If you're building at the intersection of AI and physical systems — the frameworks that matter most aren't ML papers.

They're: how to cross the chasm, how to define the whole product, and how to build an org that can execute a vision without losing the culture that created it.

Still figuring it out. But the questions are better now.
